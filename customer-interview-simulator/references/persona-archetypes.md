# Persona Archetype Character Guides

Deep character guides for each of the 5 archetypes. Use these to ensure consistent, believable behavior across different startup domains and ICP definitions.

---

## Archetype 1: The Power User

**Core identity:** This person has been waiting for someone to solve this problem. They feel the pain acutely every week and have probably Googled for solutions. When you probe, they lean in.

**Behavioral signatures:**
- Volunteers specific examples before you finish asking the question
- Mentions time lost: "I spend probably 4–5 hours a week just on this"
- Has tried things that didn't work: "We tried [tool X] for three months but it didn't..."
- Gets slightly animated when describing the frustration
- Uses language of cost and consequence: "It's caused issues with clients before"
- Asks questions back: "Is that something you're building?" / "When would this be available?"

**What they WON'T do:**
- Hedge or minimize the problem
- Give vague, one-sentence answers to problem probes
- Claim they've fully solved it

**How to write their interview:**
- Q1–2: Context-setting, slightly rushed energy — they're busy but engaged
- Q3–5: Problem probe gets specific fast. They reference a recent, concrete example.
- Q6–7: Workaround mapping reveals something elaborate and frustrating
- Q8–9: Priority is high. They've brought it up with their manager.
- Q10: Buying behavior — they've bought tools before and can move fast

**Sample language:**
- "Honestly, it's a nightmare. Just last week we had a situation where..."
- "I've tried [X], [Y], and [Z]. None of them really solved it."
- "If you could fix this, I'd probably pay for it out of my own budget."
- "Can I be on a beta list for this?"

## Archetype 2: The Pragmatist

**Core identity:** They have the problem, but they're measured. They need to see proof before changing anything. They're not skeptical of the problem — they're skeptical of solutions. ROI-driven.

**Behavioral signatures:**
- Acknowledges the problem but contextualizes it: "It's definitely not ideal..."
- Has a workaround they're mostly okay with: "We use [spreadsheet / process] and it works, mostly"
- Asks about integration and compatibility before you've even described your product
- Frames everything in terms of team impact, not personal pain
- Needs a "why now" — mentions a recent change that made the problem more acute
- Won't commit to anything; says "it would depend on..."

**What they WON'T do:**
- Get emotional or animated about the problem
- Say they're "desperate" for a solution
- Make a decision in the conversation

**How to write their interview:**
- Q1–2: Professional, measured. Describes their workflow clearly.
- Q3–5: Problem probe: they acknowledge it but rate it 6–7/10 on priority
- Q6–7: Workaround is well-described, functional but imperfect
- Q8–9: Priority calibration — it's on the list but not at the top
- Q10: Buying behavior — committee process, needs approval

**Sample language:**
- "Yeah, it's not perfect. We've just kind of built around it."
- "I mean, if something came along that integrated with [existing tool], I'd definitely take a look."
- "It's probably a 6 out of 10 priority. We have bigger fires right now."
- "I'd need to see how it works with our current stack before committing to anything."

## Archetype 3: The Skeptic

**Core identity:** They fit the ICP perfectly on paper, but the problem just isn't that real for them. Their context is slightly different — different workflow, smaller scale, or better-resourced team. They're not hostile, just genuinely unconvinced.

**Behavioral signatures:**
- Short, complete-sentence answers to problem probes
- "We don't really have that issue" — and they mean it
- Curious about what you're building (social interest) but not personally motivated
- Pivots to adjacent problems that *do* hurt them
- Rates the problem low (3–4/10) without apologizing for it
- Occasionally reveals that their context is a meaningful segment edge case

**What they WON'T do:**
- Get pulled into confirming a problem they don't have
- Suddenly "discover" the problem partway through
- Give false positive commercial signals

**How to write their interview:**
- Q1–2: Fine context. Nothing unusual.
- Q3–5: Problem probe falls flat — short answers, they don't have much to say
- Q6: Key moment — they mention something ELSE that's actually more painful. Note this.
- Q7–8: Workaround is simple because the problem is simple for them
- Q9: Priority is low. Something else is the real bottleneck.
- Q10: Wouldn't buy, but might refer someone who would

**Sample language:**
- "Honestly? We don't really run into that. Maybe it's because we're smaller."
- "I mean, it happens occasionally, but it's not something I'd call a big problem."
- "The thing that actually kills us is [different problem entirely] — that's where we spend most of our time."
- "I'd use it if it was free, but I wouldn't pay for it."

**⚡ Skill instruction:** The skeptic often accidentally surfaces adjacent problems more valuable than the founder's hypothesis. Flag these in the Interview Signal note.

## Archetype 4: The Unaware

**Core identity:** They absolutely have the problem, but they've normalized it. They don't frame it as a "problem" — it's just "how things work." They've never looked for a solution because it hasn't occurred to them that one could exist.

**Behavioral signatures:**
- Describes the workaround as standard practice: "Oh yeah, everyone does it that way"
- Uses passive language: "It just takes a while" / "You kind of have to manually..."
- Doesn't volunteer pain — you have to pull it out with specific, concrete questions
- Has an "oh huh" moment mid-interview when you probe a specific cost or frequency
- Surprised by the time/cost calculation when you reflect it back to them
- Not a natural buyer — would need significant education before commercial conversation

**What they WON'T do:**
- Lead with the problem
- Express urgency
- Make a commercial ask

**How to write their interview:**
- Q1–2: Normal workflow description — the problem is embedded in it, unremarked
- Q3: Problem probe gets a shrug. "Yeah, that's just part of the job."
- Q4–5: Probe frequency and time cost. They're slightly surprised by their own answer.
- Q6: Probe what they'd do if it was fixed. First signs of interest.
- Q7–8: Workaround is described in detail — they've optimized it without realizing they've optimized around a broken thing
- Q9: Priority is low not because it doesn't hurt but because they've never framed it as solvable
- Q10: Would try it. Wouldn't push for budget.

**Sample language:**
- "I mean, yeah, it takes a couple hours but that's just... normal, right?"
- "We just export it to a spreadsheet and work from there. Everyone does that."
- "Huh. I've never actually added that up before. That's kind of a lot."
- "I guess I never thought about fixing it. It's just how it works."

**⚡ Skill instruction:** The Unaware persona's "huh" moment is one of the most valuable signals in early discovery — it means the problem is real but the market is not yet educated. This can be an opportunity (blue ocean) or a challenge (long sales cycle to create category awareness). Flag which it is in the synthesis.

## Archetype 5: The Solved

**Core identity:** They used to have this problem acutely. They've thought hard about it and built or bought their way out of it. They're articulate, opinionated — and unlikely to switch unless you're dramatically better.

**Behavioral signatures:**
- Immediately says "yeah, we used to struggle with that" — past tense
- Describes their solution with pride and specificity
- Mentions the cost of building or customizing their current solution
- Asks technical questions: "How does it handle [edge case]?" — they know the problem space deeply
- Critical of half-measures: "We tried [common tool] and it wasn't good enough"
- Willing to refer others: "I know people who still deal with this"

**What they WON'T do:**
- Pretend they're back at square one
- Easily abandon their current solution
- Be excited by a generic pitch

**How to write their interview:**
- Q1–2: Confident, organized workflow description
- Q3–4: Problem probe — "we had that, here's how we solved it"
- Q5–6: Workaround mapping is detailed and impressive — this is where they shine
- Q7: Probe what's still not perfect. Usually there's something. This is your opening.
- Q8: Priority is low for *buying* but high for *the problem domain* — they care, they just think they've solved it
- Q9: Buying behavior — they'd look at alternatives only if you're 10x better or cheaper
- Q10: Best referral source of all 5 personas

**Sample language:**
- "Oh yeah, we dealt with that for the first two years. It was awful."
- "We ended up building an internal tool. Took about 3 months of engineering time."
- "The main thing that still isn't perfect is [specific edge case]."
- "I wouldn't switch unless you were significantly better than what we have. But I know a few people who'd love this."

**⚡ Skill instruction:** The Solved persona is critical for two reasons: (1) they confirm the problem is real enough to build solutions for, and (2) they reveal what "good enough" looks like, which sets the bar for your solution. Their residual pain points are your product differentiation brief. Always surface these in the synthesis.

## Cross-Archetype Realism Rules

Regardless of archetype, every persona must feel like a human being:

1. **Give them a life outside the problem.** They're distracted by their quarterly review, or they just hired someone new, or they're about to go on holiday. Let this bleed in occasionally.

2. **Let them be inconsistent.** Real people contradict themselves. A Pragmatist might say "it's not a huge priority" and then describe 6 hours lost per week. Note the inconsistency — it's a signal.

3. **Give them opinions about tools.** "We tried Notion for this but it was overkill." "We're a HubSpot shop — anything that doesn't integrate is a non-starter."

4. **Let them ask questions.** Even if you're not supposed to pitch, people get curious: "What are you building exactly?" A good founder would deflect. The interview should capture both the question and the deflection.

5. **Vary sentence length and register.** Power Users talk in run-ons. Skeptics use short sentences. The Unaware pauses and says "I mean..." a lot. Pragmatists speak in bullet points.
