# Discovery Interview Question Bank

Categorized question library for customer discovery interviews. Pull from all categories in each interview, weighted toward the persona's archetype and the problem domain.

---

## Category 1: Context-Setting (Open Every Interview Here)

Goal: Understand their world before probing the problem. Don't mention the problem yet.

- "Walk me through what a typical [day / week] looks like for you in your role."
- "What tools do you use most heavily right now? What's your stack for [relevant workflow]?"
- "What's the biggest thing your team is trying to get done this quarter?"
- "How long have you been in this role? What's changed about how you work since you started?"
- "How big is your team? How do you split responsibilities for [relevant function]?"
- "What does success look like in your role? How do you measure it?"

**When to use:** Always. These ground the persona and establish trust before you probe.

---

## Category 2: Problem Probing (Core Signal)

Goal: Find out if the problem exists, how often, and what it costs — without leading.

- "What takes up more time than it should in your week?"
- "What part of [relevant workflow] feels most broken right now?"
- "Tell me about the last time you had to deal with [problem area]. What happened?"
- "How often does [problem situation] come up?"
- "When [problem] happens, what do you do?"
- "What does that cost you — in time, or in other ways?"
- "Has this ever caused a real problem — a missed deadline, a lost deal, something you had to explain to someone?"
- "On a scale of one to ten, how big a priority is fixing [this area] for you right now? What's above it?"
- "Is this something you've talked to your manager / team about? What did they say?"
- "Have you ever tried to fix this? What happened?"

**When to use:** This is the core of the interview. Spend 40–50% of the session here.

**Red flags that signal weak problem:**
- "It's fine, we manage" (with no follow-up elaboration)
- Consistently rating the problem 5 or below on priority
- Long pause before answering, then a vague response — they had to think of an example

**Gold signals:**
- They volunteer an example before you finish asking
- They mention time lost, deals lost, or relationships affected
- They say "we've been meaning to fix this" — implies awareness + procrastination = real pain

## Category 3: Workaround Mapping (Competitive Intelligence)

Goal: Understand what they're doing today. This reveals switching cost and whether the problem is truly unsolved.

- "How are you handling [problem area] today?"
- "Walk me through exactly what that process looks like."
- "How long does that take you?"
- "Who else is involved in that process?"
- "What do you like about how you're doing it today? What do you wish was different?"
- "Have you tried other tools or approaches for this? What happened?"
- "What would it take for you to change how you're doing this?"
- "If your current approach disappeared tomorrow, what would you do?"
- "Are you paying for anything today to solve this, even partially?"
- "Have you ever considered building something internally for this?"

**When to use:** Always. Every persona has a workaround, even if it's "we just don't do it."

**Interpretation guide:**
- Complex, entrenched workaround (elaborate spreadsheet, hired a contractor, built internal tool) → High switching cost but real problem. Opportunity if you can be meaningfully better.
- Simple workaround that mostly works → Problem may not be acute enough. They've optimized around it.
- No workaround → Either unaware, low priority, or the problem only recently emerged.

## Category 4: Priority Calibration (Urgency Test)

Goal: Find out where this problem sits in their world relative to everything else. Urgency is the most underrated signal.

- "If you had budget to fix three things in your workflow right now, what would they be?"
- "What would have to be true for this to become your top priority?"
- "Is this something you'd solve this quarter, or is it more of a 'someday' problem?"
- "If I could wave a wand and fix this tomorrow, how different would your life look?"
- "What's the cost of *not* solving this over the next year?"
- "Is anyone in your company actively pushing to solve this? Who?"
- "What's blocking you from fixing this right now?"

**When to use:** Use 2–3 of these per interview, especially with Pragmatist, Skeptic, and Solved personas.

**Key insight:** Many founders confuse "they have the problem" with "they want to solve the problem now." Priority calibration separates the two. A problem that's real but permanently low-priority is not a business.

## Category 5: Buying Behavior (Commercial Signal)

Goal: Understand whether they have authority and appetite to buy. Only ask these late in the interview, after establishing rapport.

- "When your team adopts new tools, how does that decision usually happen?"
- "What's the approval process for new software purchases?"
- "Have you bought a tool recently to solve a workflow problem? How did that go?"
- "What do you look for when you're evaluating a new tool?"
- "What would make you say yes immediately? What would give you pause?"
- "Who else would need to be involved in a decision like this?"
- "Is there a budget set aside for tools like this, or would you have to make a case for it?"

**When to use:** Power User and Pragmatist personas. Skip or use lightly with Skeptic and Unaware — premature commercial questions break the discovery dynamic.

---

## Category 6: Closing / Referral

Goal: End with a signal on credibility and network access.

- "Is there anyone else on your team — or in your network — who deals with this more than you do?"
- "Would you be open to staying in touch as we learn more? I'd love to share what we find."
- "If we built something that solved this exactly the way you described, what would you want to see first?"

**When to use:** Close every interview with at least one of these. Referrals from believers are the most valuable output of early discovery.

## Question Quality Checklist

Before asking any question, run it through this check:

✅ Does it ask about **past behavior or current reality**? (Good)
❌ Does it ask about **future intent or hypotheticals**? (Bad — leads to false positive)

✅ Is it **open-ended**? (Good)
❌ Is it **leading** — implying the answer you want? (Bad — confirmation bias)

✅ Does it invite them to **tell a story**? (Good)
❌ Does it invite a **yes/no answer**? (Bad — gives you nothing to work with)

**The one question to avoid at all costs:**
> "Would you use a product that [describes your solution]?"

This question produces false positives almost universally. People say yes to be polite. Replace it with: *"Tell me about the last time you had to deal with [problem]."*
